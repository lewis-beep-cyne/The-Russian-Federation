Russian Economy

Russia uses the Rouble(also Ruble) as its currency.

Russia has the world's 11th largest economy, with a total GDP of over $2.4 trillion as of 2025. It is set to overtake South Korea in 2028.

Historical Background

Russia's economy has evolved dramatically over centuries. In the Tsarist era, it was largely agrarian, with peasants forming the backbone of production. Industrialization began in the late 19th century, especially under Sergei Witte’s reforms, which expanded railways and heavy industry.

The Planned Economy

The Soviet planned economy was formally enforced in 1928 under Joseph Stalin, through the First Five-Year Plan. It replaced the New Economic Policy (NEP), which had allowed limited private trade. Stalin and the Communist Party believed central planning was essential to rapidly industrialize the USSR, strengthen military power, and consolidate political control.

Gosplan, the State Planning Committee, was established to design and enforce economic plans. Agriculture was collectivized, industries were nationalized, and private ownership was abolished. This system continued until the collapse of the USSR in 1991.

Why It Was Enforced

  • To rapidly industrialize and catch up with Western powers.
  • To strengthen military capacity against external threats.
  • To enforce socialist ideology and eliminate private ownership.
  • To consolidate Communist Party control over resources and society.

Timeline of the Planned Economy

Period Event Details
1918-1921 War Communism Nationalization of industry and grain requisition during Civil War.

1928 First Five-Year Plan Stalin enforces full central planning, collectivization begins.
1930s Rapid Industrialization Steel, coal, machinery production expands; agriculture collectivized.
1940s-1980s Successive Five-Year Plans System continues, USSR becomes superpower but faces inefficiency.
1991 Collapse of USSR Planned economy ends, Russia transitions to market system.

Modern Economy

Today, Russia's economy is diversified but still heavily reliant on energy exports.

Oil, natural gas, and minerals remain its largest sources of revenue. Agriculture, defense, and technology sectors also contribute significantly.

Western sanctions since 2014 and especially after 2022 have pressured Russia's economy, but it has adapted by strengthening trade with Asia, investing in domestic industries, and promoting financial independence from Western systems.

Future Outlook

Russia's future growth will depend on balancing energy exports with innovation. Efforts are underway to expand digital technology, artificial intelligence, and renewable energy. Analysts expect Russia to remain a major global economy, with resilience shaped by its vast resources and geopolitical strategies.
